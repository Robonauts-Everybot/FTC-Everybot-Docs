Robonauts’ FTC Everybot, with a final budget of under $1000, is an affordable, competitive, and sustainable robot that can be built with nothing but basic tools, a basic 3D printer, items found in either the REV Kit of Parts, the FTC Storefront, or purchased from your local hardware store, and commonly used FTC retailers.

The dimensions in this document are nominal dimensions, unless otherwise noted.

The Everybot was built using only basic shop tools, and was designed to work with a large range of machining tolerance. The build sections are meant to be used alongside the cutlist.

The Robonauts’ Everybot Robot Will…

When deciding what this year’s Everybot would be capable of we created an exhaustive list of actions that a “Into The Deep” robot can perform and distilled that list into the Everybot capabilities. Some actions CAN be done by the robot but we will not put resources into having those capabilities as they are byproducts of the design we finished with. Below is the absolute “Robot Will” list.

In the Teleoperated Period:

  • Intake SAMPLES from the floor
  • Remove SAMPLES from inside the SUBMERSIBLE
  • Climb LOW RUNG
  • PARK
  • Score SAMPLES into LOW BASKET
  • Score SPECIMENS onto HIGH CHAMBER
  • Deliver SAMPLES to OBSERVATION ZONE
  • Score in the NET ZONE

As our prototyping and design work carried on and our absolute “Robot Will” evolved into a physical robot and the Everybot started to take shape, we got a better idea of what match play would look like for the Everybot.

Strategy

The FTC Everybot is able to contribute to any alliance that it is involved in. We identified that the two main roles will likely be a Basket specialist and a Chamber specialist. This is what drove the design, the robot must be able to play both of these roles or any combination of the two.

The robot can make Basket cycles easily by driving forwards towards the Submersible, extending the intake in to grab a Sample, and then drive backwards while lifting the arm to score in the Baskets.

The robot can make Chamber cycles by first gathering alliance specific Samples from the Submersible and placing them into the Observation Zone. Once enough have been collected and turned into Specimens, the robot will switch to scoring the Specimens. This cycle can be driving forward, parallel to the wall, into the Observation Zone to grab a Specimen. The robot can then back up and turn 90° to face the Chamber and lift the arm to score.

The Robonauts’ Everybot Design Accessibility Goals

Built with only common tools, a basic 3D printer, items purchased from your local hardware store, and online from various FTC retailers. Special emphasis was put on interchangeability of parts to further expand the accessibility of the build.

Robot Will, continued:

  • Will not use more than 8 motors
  • Will support Omni-Directional drivetrain
  • Will use custom 3D printed parts

The mission of FTC Everybot is to provide a comprehensive set of build resources to field a competitive and affordable robot intended to play the FIRST Tech Challenge; specifically targeted towards under-resourced and under-mentored teams. A successful Everybot will inspire individual students, teams, and mentors while promoting team sustainability.

The Robonauts Everybot crew is excited to be delving into FTC and is excited to take on this challenge. We also wanted to stress the importance of prototyping on your own and learning things that may not be included in this season’s Everybot build. This is partially why Everybot won’t be released until October. We hope that the Everybot will inspire teams to expand on this design to create an even more valuable asset to any alliance.

CAD software is a very useful tool for building FIRST robots. We will be designing the FTC Everybot using OnShape and intend to design the robot to be easy to iterate on and customize the design by heavily utilizing 3D printed parts.

The “Cut List”

This manual will cross-reference the ( Cut List Tab BOM & Compatibility Spreadsheet) as much as possible when calling for parts. Printing the cut list is highly recommended. To try to make the cut list and our instructions make a little more sense, here is some of the method behind the madness:

  • Stock: “stock” refers to raw material - it is the different “stuff” you are building the robot out of, like box tubing (of different dimensions), wood, or threaded rod. Each type of stock is color coded.
  • Part [.prt]: this is the name of the individual CAD computer file with the 3D model and information for this specific part of the robot.
  • Quantity: how many copies of this Part or “cut” you will need to make.
  • Length [in]: the length, in inches, of the material that this Part uses. For most types of stock the dimensions of the stock make it obvious which direction this measurement is for - it would be hard to cut either the one or two inch dimensions of 2" x 1" 1/16" Wall Tubing to be 48.62 inches long, for example.
  • Width [in]: what width to cut something to if appropriate, typically should only be used for polycarbonate sheets.
  • Note: for your own usage.
  • Purpose: which major component of the robot this piece is for.
  • Total used [in]: These measurements may help you lay out your cuts, but may also be a little confusing - don’t worry about them too much. If you make your cuts with the Quantity, Length [in], and Stick of each Part in mind they will work out.

You should clearly label machined parts in a way that makes sense to your team. We recommend writing at least the Part names from the cut list, as that will make it obvious what part to grab when this manual refers to, for example, 02_Superstructure_Upright. 3D Printing For The Everybot

3D Printer Requirements:

  1. Must be able to print parts successfully. Maintaining your tools will provide the best chances for success.
  2. Must have a minimum bed size of 200mm x 200mm (7.87” x 7.87”)

A list of some potential compatible 3D printers can be found here under the 3D Printers and Filament tab: BOM & Compatibility Spreadsheet

Desired Filament:

While many filaments can create a successful FTC Everbot, we recommend using filaments that have higher impact resistance. Some common filaments such as normal PLA & PETG do not handle impact loads very well. While materials like ABS, ASA, PC, and Nylon are good materials, they are more difficult to print. Because of this, the recommended filament type is PLA+. PLA+ and similar materials print with the ease of PLA but have material properties more similar to ABS and Nylon.
Please note that PLA is not a recommended filament.
PLA+ is much different than standard PLA. Please use a PLA+ listed in the spreadsheet provided.

A list of potential PLA+ filaments can be found here under the 3D Printers and Filament tab: BOM & Compatibility Spreadsheet

Chassis Overview

Chassis Architecture: The Everybot uses a 3D printed clamshell design for the drivebase. The clamshell parts are meant to clamp around the extrusion, eliminating the need to bolt directly to or through your desired extrusion. The chassis is designed to accommodate common drivetrain motor/gearbox combos. The drivebase also allows for an assortment of different wheel sizes, at the end of the Chassis Section, there is a table of motors, output shafts, and required hardware for different wheels.

The basic assembly steps are:

  1. Obtain/create of the following 3D printed parts:
  1. Qty(2) x 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Bottom
  2. Qty(4) x 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Top
  3. Qty (2) x 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Bottom_MIRRORED
  1. Cut extrusion to length for the chassis frame
  2. Secure clamshells to the frame
  3. Secure motors/gearboxes to clamshells with bolts and zipties
  4. Equip gearbox output shaft with desired wheel and secure wheel

If at any point you are unsure of what a direction or diagram is telling you to do, please ask for clarification in the Everybot Discord! This is the fastest way to get an answer from the Everybot team, and questions we see there will help us to revise this manual to make steps more clear for other Everybot builds.

Chassis Machining

The extrusion needs to be cut to length to create the chassis frame. We recommend that you begin printing parts before machining parts.

The 3 extrusion pieces of extrusion will be cut to a length of 300mm or 11.811” these will be the 01_Chassis_Frame_Front/Back/Battery.

2 pieces will need to be cut between 300mm and 390mm, these are the 01_Chassis_Frame_Left/Right. We recommend using the full length of 390mm if materials permit. Ensure that parts are not longer than 390mm and as close in length to each other as possible. It is better to have two at 389mm than one at 389mm and the other at 390mm.

By default the 3D printed parts expect the extrusion to be 15mm x 15mm but we do support 0.5” x 0.5” extrusion with 3D printed adapters.

For additional help and information on measurement and marking check out the Everybot Evergreens resources here!

Chassis Assembly

Start by grabbing 8 zip ties, the two 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Bottoms and their mirrored counterparts 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Bottom_MIRRORED. Thread the zip ties through the holes on the bottom, without tightening them. The zip ties are fed through the marked rectangles in the image below and will help secure the motors later on.

Next grab the 3D printed part 01_Chassis_Battery_Box and the 01_Chassis_Frame_Rear/Battery extrusion frame parts. The goal is to attach the 01_Chassis_Battery_Box in the center of the two extrusion pieces. If using the REV extrusion this step will require REV M3 hex bolts (or bolts with an equivalent profile) to be slid into the extrusion. 35mm bolts are the longest that REV offers, which will be a bit short to pop out the top of the nut, 40mm bolts would be preferred but not required.  If you are using other extrusion, then holes can be match drilled (Check out this information on Match Drilling on the Everybot Evergreen resources) or zip ties can be used. If match drilling, a 2” or 50mm bolt will work well.

Now acquire the 01_Chassis_Frame_Right extrusion, one 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Bottom, one 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Top, 8 (55-60mm/2.25-2.5”) bolts and 8 nylock nuts. If possible we recommend using the shorter length of bolt listed, as it will cut down on assembly time. Start by inserting 01_Chassis_Frame_Right into the longer channel of the bottom clamshell. Then butt the 01_Chassis_Frame_Rear/Battery extrusion against it, as shown below.

Align the top piece and clamp the clamshell down on the extrusion by adding bolts and nuts. The bolts will have their heads on the bottom with nuts on the top. When clamping down ensure that the frame does not shift. It is best practice to leave all the bolts loosely tightened then finalizing the tightening in a star like pattern, see the final diagram for a loose idea of tightening order. Note that not all bolt holes need to be used.

 

Next add the clamshell on the other side of the robot. For this you will use the MIRRORED bottom of the clamshell. Take 01_Chassis_Frame_Left and butt it up against the 3D printed part as was done previously. Then butt the two frame pieces from the previous clamshell against the extrusion. Add bolts, again tightening in a vaguely star pattern, making sure that the extrusion does not shift.

Next up are the front clamshells, which will go together the same with 3 key differences (3rd difference is on the next page):

  • There will only be one piece of horizontal extrusion, 01_Chassis_Frame_Front, which will go through the channel further away from motor mounting location.
  • The innermost bolt holes surrounding the extrusion will be left empty

Finally, we will cover how we handle different side lengths. If your sides are exactly 390mm or super close, then you will butt the ends up against the 3D printed parts as was done previously. If you are using a shorter length you will need to use the following formula to derive the offset required from the end: (390mm - YourExtrusionLengthHere = distance from end of channel). Below showcases where a 300mm side would fall relative to the back of the channel.

This distance is difficult to properly mark, if you are unable to fit a measurement tool in the channel, you could attempt to make a marking with a marker on the top and carefully translate it to the bottom. If you have a pair of calipers, you could use the inside jaws (2) to scribe a line on the side/bottom of the channel. Aftering marking it may be best to add some tape in the channel to prevent the extrusion from going deeper than desired.

With these tips in mind, we recommend placing the 01_Chassis_Frame_Front first, then adding the sides afterwards, making extra sure that the sides do not go into the shells further than desired. When ready carefully tighten everything together.

Superstructure Overview

Superstructure Architecture: The superstructure consists of uprights which host the arm plus the gearbox to move it and supports for structural integrity. A good chunk superstructure steps will require completion of the chassis but please note that the arm and intake can be built separately. The top level construction/assembly steps are:

  1. Start 3D printing all required parts if you have not already done so
  2. Machine the 02_Superstructure_Uprights and 02_Superstructure_Supports
  3. Attach the 02_Superstructure_Foot_L/R to the chassis
  4. Mount the 02_Superstructure_Uprights
  5. Put 02_Superstructure_Supports into 02_Superstructure_Foot_L/R and clamp to Uprights with 02_Superstructure_Brace_Mounts
  6. Slide 02_Superstructure_Gearbox_Mount and 02_Superstructure_Gear_Support onto Uprights
  7. Add U-Bolts to top of the Uprights

The gearbox and pinion gear will be added later on down the line.

Superstructure Machining

We will need to cut two 02_Superstructure_Supports and two 02_Superstructure_Uprights. The supports are 225mm[8.85”] long and the uprights are 327mm[12.875”] long.
You may want to cut the uprights a bit shorter if you use wheels of a larger diameter. You should remove whatever the difference in radius of the wheel is to ensure the arm is at the right height.

Drilling Tips and Tricks

For these drilling operations, it is more important the holes line up when the bottoms of the extrusion are even, than it is that each individual hole is in the perfect place. To help us ensure these holes are even with each other, we will use a technique called match drilling. Match drilling is a drilling operation wherein holes are drilled through multiple stacked components at once, with the goal of ensuring that the holes line up perfectly between the two pieces. If you were trying to draw a circle by hand, a stencil would help to make the circle more accurate, and similar techniques can be used to drill holes. If you need to drill out holes to mount a bracket or plate and the bracket can be clamped down on top of the workpiece, the holes can be drilled into the workpiece through the holes in the bracket to ensure they will line up.

Here we can use up to two types of match drilling, the first type is showcased above, with an existing object being used as a template to transfer holes. The other type occurs between two pieces of extrusion that must be identical, make sure to line them on one end only. Think about what you will be attaching the tube stock to and align the parts on the end that will be attached to the more “solid” component (for example, the side that will be attached to the chassis versus having a mechanism or more superstructure attached. It is best if this side is a factory edge, as filed edges may not be as easy to line up. Let's look at our case, we are looking at the vertical uprights:

Here it looks like the U-bolts are the two top bolts, while the bottom bolt on the side supports the structure. Therefore we will look to line up the bottoms of the extrusion when drilling. If we lined the top and the extrusions are not exactly the same length, then we could end up with an upright that is too long to line up the bolt hole in the chassis or too short to properly sit on the bottom of the chassis extrusion cutout.

Drilling The Holes

Now that we have covered some concepts let's get to drilling. Here are the sizes of hole we will need depending on your hardware:

  • On the side for the single smaller hole:
  • 3mm for M3 (4mm may also work fine but will be a bit oversized)
  • 4mm for M4 (5mm may also work fine but will be a bit oversize)
  • #27/25 or 5/32 for 6-32 (recommend against 5/32th as it will be a bit oversized, #27 will be a closer fit, which might make installation of hardware slightly more difficult, #25 is good)
  • #18/16 or 11/64 for 8-32 (#18 and 11/64ths will be closer fits, any size here is good with #16 being good for the wiggle room it provides)
  • On the front for the two larger holes:
  • ¼ hole for ¼-20” U-bolts

 We will start with the easier method which requires you to print the 02_Superstructure_Drillling_Guide. Skip the next page if you did not print this and find the image of the tube stock in the red vise.

The drilling guide features undersized holes that can be used to create pilot holes in the extrusion, which can be finished off with the correct drill size. When using the drill guide, make sure to only create pilot holes on a single piece of extrusion. We need the holes to line up nicely across the uprights and if they are slightly different lengths this will lead to some error. To figure out which side of your extrusion is the most flat, this will be bottom. Ideally this will be factory edge. Place the guide on the bottom and drill out the single small side hole. Ideally you will be able to find a drill bit that nicely fits in the hole but a slightly larger drill bit will also work nicely, just make sure it is not larger than the hole being drilled. Then keeping the guide in the same orientation, place on top and drill the two larger holes. Use the information on top to ensure you don’t drill holes larger than their final size. We highly recommend using a marker to label the top, bottom, front and side of the extrusion.

Top

Bottom

When you finish drilling the pilot holes, widen the holes to their correct size. Remember the side hole will receive your typical hardware bolt size (M3, M4, 6-32 or 8-32) and the front top holes will want holes for the U-bolts (very likely ¼” in the USA).

Next you will line up the bottoms of your upright extrusion, making sure to select the flatter edge of your undrilled extrusion. When lined up, clamp together with C-clamps or use a vise then transfer the holes, making sure to rotate 90 degrees when switching holes sizes. When rotating MAKE SURE TO ALIGN THE BOTTOMS AND NOT THE TOPS.

Example of tube stock after a match drilling operation

If you are not using the drilling guide, you will want a set of calipers to scribe the lines and a center punch. With the calipers you will: zero them with the jaws against each other, open them until you see your desired length, then turn the wheel to lock them in place and use them to scratch a line into your extrusion. Two lines will create a point that can be center punched to ensure your drill will start in the correct location. On an extrusion that lacks a flat surface this may be tough, you should be able to scribe the distance from the bottom, then try your best to center the hole.

example of center punched points for holes to be drilled

Superstructure Assembly

Start by grabbing the 02_Superstructure_Foot_L/R. These will be attached to the 01_Chassis_Clamshell_Tops in the front of the robot. These will be attached by two bolts each, one bolt will be 60mm[2.5”] and the other is a 75mm[3.0”] bolt. Please note that the feet have letters to indicate which side they belong to. When the robot is viewed with the battery bracket furthest away from you is how we define left and right. See the image below for verification that you have correctly equipped the robot with its feet.


There is a good chance that the shorter of the two bolts will interfere with the support, especially if it is not the REV extrusion. First try inserting the 02_Superstructure_Support into the foot to test for interference. If there is an issue, remove one foot and insert the support. The foot will be used as a guide for your drill, using a smaller drill bit to make a hole in the extrusion, with the extrusion all the way in the tube. Then using a larger drill bit widen until the interference is fixed.

After ensuring that the supports have no interference, we will need to attach the 02_Superstructure_Uprights to the chassis. Use 30mm[1.125-1.25”] (longer imperial preferred, especially with washers) bolts to attach the uprights, with the nylock nuts in the 3D slot and bolts on the outside. You may need to slide the battery bracket if you are having trouble getting a tool in.

Now the uprights need to be supported with the 02_Superstructure_Supports. Start by inserting the supports in the feet then grab the four 02_Superstructure_Brace_Mounts. Clamshell the supports to the uprights using the brace mounts. Fasten with four 40mm[1.5”/ 1.625”/1.75”] bolts (1.5” might be just short with washers), with nylock nuts on the inside of the robot.

Next attach the 02_Superstructure_Gearbox_Mount and the 02_Superstructure_Gear_Support to the uprights. These can rest loosely on top of the brace mounts, as their placement will be finalized later. The bolts can be inserted now though, just make sure not to tighten. The gearbox mount uses two 50mm[2.0”] or longer bolts while the gear support uses a 30mm[1.125”] or longer bolt. Both nuts will face towards the center of the chassis. Ensure that the mounts are in the correct orientation, see the picture below for this.

 

Finally comes the installation of the U-bolts. The bolts attached to the U should point away from the robot. It’s likely that your u-bolt won’t fit with the holes you drilled. Either because of bad hole placement or the fact that all U-bolts are bent to random angles and none are the same. We found using a pair of channel locks or sufficiently large pliers to squeeze the U-bolt during installation made it much easier. When installing, if your U-bolt came with a plate, make sure that touches the extrusion on the nut side. The U-bolts will be tightened using two non-nylock nuts. The U-bolts can be left loose for now, we will return to the superstructure once the arm is completed.

Arm Overview

Arm Architecture: The arm consists of several large 3D printed parts, one of which carries a herringbone gear that rotates the arm. Another 3D printed part carries a motor which uses a wheel to push the “extension” in and out via moving a tape measure. The drawer slide elevator hosts the final subsystem: the intake. The arm also houses the hooks used by the Everybot to climb. The top level construction/assembly steps are:

  1. Cut 03_Arm_PVC_Pivot and 03_Arm_PVC_Backstop to length
  2. Cut felt pad to length and a drill hole in it, THEN stick the felt pad down
  3. Remove belt clip from tape measure
  4. Press bearing into 03_Arm_Herringbone
  5. Attach drawer slide with low profile nylock nuts and shortest bolts possible
  6. Press fit 3D printed hex shaft onto extension motor output
  7. Equip the 03_Arm_Gearbox_Mount with desired gearbox then output shaft with wheels and 3D printed parts
  8. Extend tape of tape measure and install the 03_Arm_Gearbox_Mount with bolts and nuts
  9. Zip tie tape measure in place
  10. Attach 03_Arm_Hooks
  11. Slide 03_Arm_PVC_Pivot into the U-bolts, 03_Arm_PVC_Spacers and arm assembly, squeeze the uprights together and secure in place with U-bolts
  12. Attach gearbox to 02_Superstructure_Gearbox_Mount, add pinion and pinion support
  13. Fully mesh arm gears and tighten 02_Superstructure_Gearbox_Mount and 02_Superstructure_Gear_Support into position
  14. Add PVC backstop

Arm Machining

The arm will require 2 pieces of PVC. One is 03_Arm_PVC_Pivot at 216mm[8.5”] and the other is 03_Arm_PVC_Backstop at 254mm[10.0”]. There will also be 3 pieces of threaded rod cut to 100mm[4.0”] if you don’t have bolts at least this long (3.75” bolts will work though).

A felt pad must be cut to size, this requires that you have printed the 03_Arm_Herringbone (herringbone is the type of double helix gear used on the arm). Start by placing your tape measure against the rear of the slot showcased below.

Put the felt against the tape measure and make markings on the unfuzzy side where the felt meets the 3D printed part, the goal is to have it fit nicely in this 3D printed part. Cut the felt to length and place it in the herringbone once more, making sure to NOT remove the tape. Use a marker to create a dot on the underside of the it via the highlighted hole:

Use an oversized drill bit to drill out a hole in the bottom of the felt. 6mm[0.25”] is a good size. The felt can now be stuck to the part, making sure that the drilled hole presides over the 3D printed hole.

felt installation location

Depending on the your chosen pivot motor, the motor which will control the position of your arm, you will need a different support shaft. For an HD Hex motor you will need 5mm hex support 104mmm[4.125”], for the NeveRest 40 and MAX TorqueNARDO you will need a 6mm D 120mm[4.75”] shaft and for Yellow Jacket you will need 130mm[5”] of 8mm REX or hex.

Arm Assembly

To start we will need to use a flanged 12mm OD 8mm ID 3.5mm width bearing into the 03_Arm_Herringbone in the spot shown below. To press this use your hands (preferred), a C-clamp, table vise or arbor press. Be very careful not to exert too much force, this is a 3D printed part after all. It may be tempting but it is best practice to not hammer bearings into place (a rubber mallet can be used if you must).

Then remove the belt clip from your tape measure. Most tape measures use a single philips head screw.

Next we will look to attach the drawer slide. Start by removing the rubber piece at the end that keeps a drawer shut when using the drawer slide for its actual intended purpose.

Next attach the drawer slide. For this you will need low profile nylock nut and button head bolts. It is imperative that the low profile hardware is used. Otherwise, the extension will not function correctly. The low profile nylock nuts will go on the tape measure side, with the button head bolts on the drawer slide side.The button head bolts will need to be 11mm [7/16”]. I did have trouble finding a low profile M3/M4. It may be difficult to fasten the nut into the felt pad but it is imperative that it looks like the image shown below.

Next will come the press fitting of the 3D printed part to your shaft. We have different shafts depending on your chosen motor. The HD Hex Motor 20:1 Planetary Gearbox will need the 03_Arm_5mm_Converter, the NeveRest Orbital 20 Gearmotor will require the 03_Arm_6mmD_Converter, the 24mm long 8mm Rex Yellow Jacket will require the 03_Arm_8mm_Converter_for_24mmREX and the 80mm long 8mm Rex Yellow Jacket uses 03_Arm_8mmREX_80mm_Shaft_Converter. If you are having trouble pushing the adapter onto the end of the motor, heating up the shaft with a lighter or heat gun may help it slide on easier.

Next you will want to mount your chosen gearmotor to the 03_Arm_Gearbox_Mount. The Yellow Jacket will require two 20mm M4s and use the uncompleted circles. The HD Hex Motor 20:1 Planetary Gearbox will require two 15mm M3s and the same goes for the NeveRest 20 two with the bolts both using the completed circles. Use a zip tie to secure the motor once in place with the grove in the rightmost image.

Once the shaft is on, add the following hardware to the 3D printed shaft on your motor in this order: 03_Arm_Tophat_Shaft_Spacer, two compliant wheels and finally 03_Arm_Bull_Nose_Shaft_Spacaer. Squish them together, with the tophat being fairly close to the motor, the goal will be to roughly center the wheels with the tape measure.

Extend the tape measure about 8” ensuring that the clip on the end clears the felt, lock this in place. With the hardware installed the 03_Arm_Gearbox_Mount can be meshed with the bearing on the 03_Arm_Herringbone and bolted together with 75-80mm/3” bolts and nylock nuts. If using 6-32s or M3s make sure to use washers on the bolt heads. DO NOT OVERTIGHTEN, once the bolt has popped through the nylock it just needs to be flush against the surface. Make sure to leave the marked bolt holes empty, those will be used for the hooks later on:

 

Once the motor is installed and the pivot is almost complete, it is okay to unlock the tape measure. Even if it’s still floating in its spot. We will mount it next. Using 2 zip ties, maybe 3, maybe one really long one, loop the zip tie through the parts and above the tape measure. (Zip ties in purple)


From here, the 03_Arm_Hooks will be attached in the 3 empty bolt holes. If using bolts 100mm[4.0”] bolts make sure to not overtighten and to use washers with 6-32s/M3s. If using threaded rod you will have nylock nuts on both sides but it may be awkward to tighten both nuts evenly. For the best odds make sure to hand tighten the nylock nuts on both sides before involving wrenches, try to ensure the bolt penetrates the nylock on both sides but be careful not to over tighten. Using a pair of vice-grips to hold the threaded rod while tightening the nylock nuts might be helpful.

The arm is ready to attach to the superstructure, if you haven’t finished the superstructure yet, the intake can be started. Before attaching the arm to the superstructure we need to take a few steps to prepare the superstructure.

Preparing Superstructure for the Arm

First we must attach the motor/gearbox that will control the rotation of the arm. As of the time of writing we do not support the 80mm output shaft from the Yellow Jacket motor. There are 3 main configurations, with the NeveRest and MAX TorqueNado sharing the same layout:


All motors will want 2-3x 10mm M3s except the Yellow Jacket, which wants 2x 10mm M4s. Afterwards slide in and tighten the two zip ties around the motor.

Next comes the 02_Superstructure_YourOutputShaftSizeHere_Pinion which will go on the output shaft of your motor. The 5mm and 8mm pinions can be added right away but the 6mm D pinion will require some additional preparation. If you are struggling to put on the pinion, heat up the output shaft with a heat gun or lighter. For the 6mm D you will need to use an additional 64mm[2.5”] 6mm D shaft. This should stay in on its own but feel free to hot glue it in.

Now you will run your 02_Superstructure_Support_Shaft through the 02_Superstructure_Gear_Support then into the 02_Superstructure_Pinion. Add a shaft collar on the gear support side with the shaft flush with end. A second shaft collar will be added later on down the line to keep the pinion in our desired location.

Connecting the Arm to the Superstructure

At this point the gear support and gearbox mount should be loose on the superstructure’s uprights. When ready grab your assembled arm, two 03_Arm_Super_Spacers, two 03_Arm_PVC_Endcaps, and the 03_Arm_PVC_Pivot. Have someone hold the arm in between the U-bolts then slide the PVC through the U-bolt, a spacer, the arm, another spacer and the U-bolt on the other side. Once in place squeeze the uprights together and tighten the U-bolts to their final tightness. A nylock is preferred but tightening two regular bolts against each other also works.

From here, simultaneously move the gear support and gearbox mount of the superstructure up the extrusion towards the arm. In order for the herringbone gears to properly mesh the center of the grooves must line up. If they do not, shift your pinion gear until it is lined up. Now attempt to mesh the pinion to arm, rotate the arm to get the best mesh. When you are confident in your meshing, tighten the bolts on the superstructure's 3D printed parts. The 2nd shaft collar can now be added against the pinion.

From here add the 03_Arm_PVC_Backstop to the upper PVC hole and add tape to act as shaft collars. This will prevent the arm from over rotating.

Lastly add a badge clip, this will be used to manage the cable for your servo motor on the intake.

Intake Overview

Intake Architecture: The intake is a side roller style intake, with wheels on the sides and a centered backstop. The arms holding the intake wheels are sprung together

  1. Cut shafts to length
  2. Attach 04_Intake_Middle_Body to 04_Intake_Drawer_Mount
  3. Put desired servo and accompanying hardware on 04_Intake_Upper_Body
  4. Add bearings to everything
  5. Attach 04_Intake_Lower_Body to 04_Intake_Middle_Body with nuts on the inside of the frame
  6. Put shaft collars on the bottom of all shafts and create hardware stack before sprockets are reached

  9. Attach 04_Intake_Upper_Body to 04_Intake_Middle_Body with nuts on the inside of the frame
  10. Zip tie in tape measure

Intake Machining

Cut two 5mm hex shafts to a length of 85mm[3.375”] and two more 5mm hex to 90mm[3.5”]. If either of these shafts are a bit longer it shouldn’t be an issue.

Intake Assembly

Start by attaching the 04_Intake_Middle_Body to the 04_Intake_Drawer_Mount. This will require two 35mm[1.375”] bolts and one 40-45mm[1.625”] (45mm preferred) bolt. The upper two bolts will face away from the drawer mount, while the bottom bolt will face the opposite direction, with a nut on the side.

Next press most of the bearings into place, two will go in the 04_Intake_Lower_Body, two per 04_Intake_Bottom_Pincer and two per 04_Intake_Upper_Pincer.

Next attach the 04_Intake_Lower_Body to 04_Intake_Middle_Body with 25mm[1.0”] bolts and nylock nuts on the inside of the frame.

 

Now we will start building up our hex shaft stacks, starting by attaching 5mm shaft collars to all intake hex shafts. The front two 85mm[3.375”] hex shafts will also need 04_Intake_Shaft_Caps, slide the collar into the cap then attach for these. The shaft collars work by tightening a set screw against the surface of the shaft, so make sure your set screws are lined up with the flats and not the corners of the hex.

 

The 90mm[3.5”] hex shafts will be equipped with 60T gears. The shorter 85mm[3.375”] capped shaft will be equipped with a 2” compliant wheel then a 15mm hex spacer.

It may be easiest to lay the main intake assembly on its side for these next steps. Insert the short capped shafts into 04_Intake_Lower_Pincer bodies. The shaft will go on the side that lacks a half circle on the end.

The other shaft will then go through the main intake assembly, with the half circle in the back and the downward extruded triangle on the correct sides.

Next you will need to add the sprockets. This will be easiest to do with the chain already strung between the sprockets. We will be using #25 roller chain, which have a link every 0.25”, we need 10.5” of chain, which will equate to 42 links, it is easiest to measure the length though. We will need two chains of this length. With the recommended VEX IQ chain, it can be broken and put back together by hand, you just need to bend it a bit. If you are using metal #25 chain here is a small guide on breaking chain:

What you might normally think about as a link of chain actually comes in two variants - an “inside” link and an “outside” link. If you look closely at the roller chain, you will see that the “sides” of each alternating link are either inside or outside of the two next to it.

Looking at the #25 roller chain, find the first section of chain that has pins through both holes of an outer section (in the photo above, this would be the second link in from the left). Set up the chain in the #25 chain tool so that the pin closer to the free end of the chain in this outer section is lined up with the channel cut towards the bottom of the tool (usually towards the left of the tool). The shorter bolt that extends into the channel cut upwards into the tool (usually to the right) is tightened down to the chain to hold it in place (and used in reassembling the chain later) and the longer bolt with a pin attached is tightened down onto the chain to push the pin in the chain links out. Some chain tools also have a third shorter bolt that can be inserted into the bottom of the downwards channel to prevent a pin from being fully knocked out - insert this if your chain tool has it. Begin tightening the pin bolt down so it starts to push the pin in the links of the chain out of the upper outer plate and into the lower channel. If your tool does not have the additional bolt in the lower channel to prevent a pin from being fully pushed out, back out the pin bolt often to check when the short, unwanted section of chain is finally free before the pin is fully pushed out of the bottom outer plate of the link we want. If the pin does pop out of the outermost plate, try again on the next full outer plate. This pin will be pushed back into an inside link.

Measure out 10.5” of chain, then break the chain, making sure to pop the pin out of an inside link. Then remove the bolt previously used to hold the chain in place from the tool and reposition the chain inside the tool with the pin now sitting in the upwards channel. Place the “inside” link inside the outer plates of the chain so that the pin will trap it in place when it is pushed back in. Retighten the locking bolt down so the “cup” at the end of the bolt starts to push the pin back through the outer plate of the chain, and then switch to the longer pin bolt to fully reinsert the pin through both outer plates.

(picture shows a chain tensioner instead of the same chain)

With the chain together, take a 10 and 20 tooth sprocket and mesh the chain such that they are as far apart as possible. With one person holding this on top of the bottom pincer, move the hex shaft through the sprockets. The 10T sprocket will be at the front of the intake with the 20T at the back. Those using the VEX chain may find it easier to skewer the sprockets with a shaft then add the chain after.

Now add the 04_Intake_Upper_Pincer_Body on top of the sprockets, making sure that 3 slots face outwards from the center of the intake. Then shaft collars can be added to the tops, making sure that the hex shaft is as high as possible with the rear longer hex shaft, although it is important to not compress anything which would introduce unwanted friction.

Now add 40mm[1.5”] bolts through the holes of the bottom pincers and secure them with nuts on the top pincers.

Press one more bearing into the 04_Intake_Upper_Body. From here, you will need to add your servo. Start by resting the servo pinion in the hole surrounded by the ¾ rounded rectangle.

Then add 35mm[1.375”] bolts to secure the servo, you probably only need two. The holes are tapered so your bolts will thread into the part, so nuts are not needed but can be used on the bottom of the servo if you want them.

Next mesh the hex pinion adapter to servo and then add the 04_Intake_Upper_Body to the whole assembly. You can also attempt to mesh the hex first then the servo, whatever you find easiest. The top will be mounted just like the bottom with two 25mm[1.0”] bolts on top with nuts in the middle windows.

Attach the intake to the drawer slide with three 25mm[1.0”] button head bolts on the drawer side and nylock nuts on the printed side.

From here the tape measure will need to be zip tied into the 04_Intake_Drawer_Mount.

Power cables from top to bottom:

  • Port 1: Rear left drive motor (driver's side if looking at it like a car)
  • Port 2: Extension motor
  • Port 3: Rear right drive motor (passengers side)

The other XT30 is connected to the XT30 Power Distribution Block

There are many different choices that can be made here. Instead of using a XT30 Power Distribution device and Spark Mini motor controllers, an Expansion hub could be used instead. For more information on how to use a setup besides the one we used, please refer to https://ftc-docs.firstinspires.org/en/latest/control_hard_compon/rc_components/index.html.

Start by mounting your REV Control Hub. We mounted ours using M3 bolts to the 02_Superstructure_Supports. If you have an expansion hub, that should work nicely on the other support. We used the channels in the REV extrusion for our bolts but if you need to drill holes to mount yours. Take extra care to ensure that there are no loose metal shavings or chips that could fall into your Control Hub.

Install the battery pack into the battery bracket on the chassis. It is best practice to secure your battery so that it cannot come loose, especially during collisions or climbing, velcro will work well for this but zip ties can be used in a pinch.

Mount the power switch in your desired location. We decided that the vertical upright was the best spot for our robot. Next attach your power switch between the battery connector and the Control Hub.

Plug the XT30 Power Distribution Block into the Control Hub and zip tie it to the side extrusion of the chassis. (01_Chassis_Frame_Side)

Plug in Spark Mini into PWM port 1 and into the front left drive motor.

Plug in Spark Mini into PWM port 2 and into the front right drive motor. (ignore the zip tie location)

Zip tie the motor controllers behind the front extrusion crossbar (01_Chassis_Frame_Front)

Plug the rear left drive motor into Port 1 on the Control Hub

Plug the rear right drive motor into Port 3 on the Control Hub

There are many ways to clean up the wiring for the rear drive motors.This is all that we did on ours.

Plug the Extension motor into Port 2 on the Control Hub and run the wire below the battery tray and up the opposite side vertical upright. Be sure to leave enough slack at the end of the wire to allow the arm to fully rotate through its range of motion without snagging the wire.

When running any wires to the arm, it is best to test that there will be no snags or interferences by repeatedly running the arm all the way up and down.

You may need to put an extended wire, or a wire coupler to get a long enough wire to run all the way to the extension motor. We used a REV JST VH 2-pin Joiner Board and placed it just opposite of the battery tray.

Next, we will plug in the Intake Servo. Plug the PWM extension wire into PWM Port 0 on the Control Hub. Be sure to run the wire in a way that will not impede the function of the robot. We have included pictures below of where we ziptied the wire and hooked the retractable badge clip. Again, make sure to run the arm and the extensions through their range of motion to ensure that the wire does not snag.

Finally, plug in the Arm Motor into Port 0 on the Control Hub. This is the only motor where the code relies on the encoder. Ensure that you plug in the encoder wire into both the motor and the Control Hub.
